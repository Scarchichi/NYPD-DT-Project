# Public Policy Case Study Guidance

Adapted from Harvard Kennedy School case methodology for political leadership and international development cases.

---

## How HKS Cases Differ from HBS Cases

| Element | HBS Business Case | HKS Public Policy Case |
|---------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **Protagonist** | Manager/executive with P&L responsibility | Public official with policy authority |
| **Core Question** | "What should the company do?" | "What decision must the official make?" |
| **Stakeholders** | Shareholders, customers, employees, competitors | Citizens, political actors, international partners, affected populations |
| **Constraints** | Resources, market dynamics, competition | Political feasibility, legal authority, institutional capacity, public opinion |
| **Success Metrics** | Profitability, market share, ROI | Policy effectiveness, legitimacy, equity, public welfare |
| **Time Horizon** | Quarters, fiscal years | Electoral cycles, generational change |
| **Accountability** | Board, shareholders | Voters, courts, international bodies |

## Essential HKS Case Elements

### 1. The Decision Maker Frame

Every HKS case must clearly establish:

1. **Who is the decision maker?**
   - Their formal authority and informal power
   - What they can and cannot do legally/constitutionally
   - Their political capital and constraints

2. **What specific decision must they make?**
   - The decision should be concrete, not abstract
   - There should be a time pressure or forcing mechanism
   - The consequences of action vs. inaction should be clear

3. **What is the environment in which they operate?**
   - Institutional constraints (laws, procedures, bureaucracy)
   - Political constraints (opposition, allies, public opinion)
   - Resource constraints (budget, personnel, time)
   - International constraints (treaties, pressure, dependencies)

### 2. Stakeholder Analysis

Public policy cases require systematic mapping of actors:

**Key Questions:**
- Whose support does the protagonist need?
- Who can veto or block action?
- Who will be affected by the decision?
- What coalitions are possible?

### 3. Values Trade-offs (Not Just Strategy Trade-offs)

HKS cases often involve genuine tensions between competing values:

**Common Value Tensions in Public Policy:**
- Efficiency vs. Equity
- Speed vs. Due Process
- Security vs. Liberty
- Sovereignty vs. Integration
- Accountability vs. Flexibility
- Short-term relief vs. Long-term reform
- Democratic legitimacy vs. Expert judgment
- National interest vs. International obligations

**Writing Guidance:**
- Name the tension explicitly
- Show evidence for both sides
- Do NOT resolve the tension
- Discussion questions should force students to choose and defend

### 4. Institutional Context

Public policy cases require more institutional detail than business cases:

**Include:**
- Formal powers and procedures
- Informal norms and precedents
- Bureaucratic capacity (or lack thereof)
- Judicial review mechanisms
- Electoral/political accountability structures
- International institutional context (EU, UN, WTO, etc.)

**The "Implementation Question":**
- Even if a decision is made, can it actually be executed?
- What bureaucratic/political obstacles exist?
- Who must cooperate for success?

## Adapting the Four-Document Structure

### Main Case Document: Public Policy Modifications

**Opening:**
- Start with a decision moment, not just a business challenge
- Establish the stakes for affected populations
- Show the constraints on the protagonist's authority

**Country/Institutional Context (replaces "Company Context"):**
- Historical background relevant to the decision
- Political system and power dynamics
- Economic context and dependencies
- International relationships and obligations

**The Challenge:**
- Frame as a policy problem, not just a management problem
- Show why standard approaches won't work
- Identify the forcing mechanism (why must they decide now?)

**Strategic Decisions:**
- For each decision, address:
  - What legal/political authority enabled it?
  - What opposition did it face?
  - What stakeholders were affected?
  - What values trade-off did it involve?

**Current State:**
- What remains unresolved?
- What new challenges have emerged?
- How has the political landscape changed?

### Technical Supplement: Public Policy Modifications

**Add sections for:**
- **Political System Overview** - How does government work in this context?
- **Legal/Constitutional Framework** - What authorities and limits exist?
- **International Context** - Treaties, alliances, dependencies
- **Historical Background** - Path dependencies that constrain options
- **Comparative Cases** - How did other countries handle similar issues?

**Frameworks should include:**
- Stakeholder analysis frameworks
- Policy implementation models
- Political feasibility analysis
- International relations theories (if relevant)

### Teaching Note: Public Policy Modifications

**Learning Objectives typically include:**
- Analyze leadership strategies for [policy domain]
- Evaluate trade-offs between [competing values]
- Apply frameworks for [analytical skill]
- Assess the role of [institution/actor] in [policy outcome]

**Discussion Guide additions:**
- Stakeholder analysis exercise (map actors on board)
- Values trade-off debate (assign students to positions)
- Implementation feasibility assessment
- Comparative case discussion (what can be learned from other contexts?)

**Closing questions should address:**
- What can other [countries/leaders/institutions] learn?
- What aspects are context-specific vs. generalizable?
- What conditions would change your recommendation?

## Common Pitfalls in Public Policy Cases

### 1. Advocacy Disguised as Analysis
- **Wrong:** "Sandu's decision to expedite judicial procedures was necessary"
- **Right:** "The decision to expedite judicial procedures raised tensions between speed and due process"

### 2. Ignoring Institutional Constraints
- **Wrong:** "The president decided to change the constitution"
- **Right:** "The president proposed a constitutional referendum, requiring majority approval"

### 3. Single-Actor Focus
- **Wrong:** Treating the protagonist as a free agent
- **Right:** Showing how the protagonist must work through/around other actors

### 4. Western-Centric Framing
- **Wrong:** Assuming EU/US perspectives are neutral benchmarks
- **Right:** Acknowledging that international actors have their own interests

### 5. Teleological Narratives
- **Wrong:** "The reforms led inevitably to success"
- **Right:** "The reforms succeeded in [X] but created new challenges in [Y]"

## Discussion Question Templates for Public Policy

### Stakeholder Analysis
- "What were the objectives of [actor]? How did they conflict with [protagonist's] goals?"
- "Which stakeholders had to be neutralized vs. co-opted vs. defeated?"

### Values Trade-offs
- "Was [protagonist's] decision to [action] a necessary compromise or a dangerous precedent?"
- "How should leaders balance [value A] against [value B] when both cannot be fully achieved?"

### Implementation
- "What institutional factors enabled [outcome]? What would have prevented it?"
- "Could this approach work in [different context]? What would need to be different?"

### Generalization
- "What elements of this approach are transferable? What is context-specific?"
- "What conditions would have led to a different outcome?"

### Leadership
- "What personal qualities did the situation require? Were these the same qualities needed for other challenges?"
- "How did [protagonist] build political capital for this decision?"

## Sources for Public Policy Context

When researching public policy cases, include:

1. **Government/Official Sources**
   - Official websites, statements, legal documents
   - Parliamentary debates, committee reports
   - Court decisions (if relevant)

2. **Think Tank Analysis**
   - Policy-focused research (RAND, Brookings, CFR, CSIS, etc.)
   - Regional specialists (European Council on Foreign Relations, etc.)

3. **International Organization Reports**
   - IMF, World Bank economic analyses
   - UN agency reports
   - Regional body assessments (EU Commission reports, etc.)

4. **Journalistic Accounts**
   - Quality international reporting
   - Local language sources (with translation)
   - Investigative journalism on key events

5. **Academic Research**
   - Peer-reviewed political science, public policy journals
   - Area studies scholarship
