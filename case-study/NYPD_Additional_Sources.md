# Additional Sources Document

## Purpose
This document organizes the source material used in the NYPD Domain Awareness System case. It provides a bibliography of confirmed references, a set of key quotes organized by theme, a data points table with source attribution, and a timeline of major events relevant to DAS.

- **Source**: INFORMS Journal on Applied Analytics
- **URL**: https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/inte.2019.0985

- **Description**: The original peer-reviewed paper describing the Patternizr algorithm developed by NYPD data scientists and published in 2019. ### 5. Keeping NYC Streets Safe for All: NYPD Domain Awareness System
- **Source**: INFORMS Franz Edelman Award Case Study

- **Description**: Franz Edelman Award case study recognizing DAS as a high-impact application of operations research and analytics. ### 6. Documents Uncover NYPD's Vast License Plate Reader Database
- **Source**: American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU)
- **URL**: https://www.aclu.org/news/privacy-technology/documents-uncover-nypds-vast-license-plate-reader-database
- **Date accessed**: April 26, 2026
- **Type**: Civil liberties report
- **Description**: Investigative report published in 2016, drawing on documents obtained through public records requests, that reveals the full scale of NYPD's automated license plate reader program. Describes the collection and retention of location data on millions of innocent motorists and frames the privacy stakes of long-term mass tracking as a structural civil liberties concern.

### 7. Automatic License Plate Readers
- **Source**: New York Civil Liberties Union (NYCLU)
- **URL**: https://www.nyclu.org/report/automatic-license-plate-readers
- **Date accessed**: April 26, 2026
- **Type**: Civil liberties report
- **Description**: Comprehensive NYCLU analysis of New York's license plate reader infrastructure, examining how LPR data is collected, stored, and shared across agencies. Argues that the geographic concentration of readers and the absence of meaningful retention limits create conditions for systematic surveillance of residents in specific communities.

### 8. New York City Police Department Surveillance Technology
- **Source**: Brennan Center for Justice
- **URL**: https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/new-york-city-police-department-surveillance-technology
- **Date accessed**: April 26, 2026
- **Type**: Investigative report
- **Description**: Brennan Center research report cataloging the full range of surveillance tools deployed by the NYPD, including DAS, cell-site simulators, facial recognition pilots, and social media monitoring platforms. Identifies significant governance gaps, transparency deficits, and the structural absence of independent oversight mechanisms across the department's technology portfolio.

### 9. An Evaluation of the NYPD's Patternizr
- **Source**: Fordham Urban Law Journal
- **URL**: https://ir.lawnet.fordham.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=2779&context=ulj
- **Date accessed**: April 26, 2026
- **Type**: Peer-reviewed article
- **Description**: Legal and analytical evaluation of Patternizr that examines the system's constitutional implications, potential for racial bias amplification, and procedural safeguards requirements. Argues that algorithmic crime analytics tools trained on historical police data require ongoing independent audit and transparent methodology disclosure to ensure legally and ethically defensible outcomes.

## Timeline of Significant Events
- **2005**: NYPD launches the Real Time Crime Center, an early data warehouse and command center that becomes the foundation for later DAS development.
- **2008**: Lower Manhattan Security Initiative deploys integrated cameras and sensors in the financial district, demonstrating early sensor fusion capabilities.
- **2012**: NYPD and Microsoft publicly announce the citywide rollout of the Domain Awareness System.
- **2014**: DAS expands with broader mobile access and AVL integration for more than 5,000 police vehicles.
- **2016�2017**: Public scrutiny grows as investigative reporting highlights concerns about racial profiling, camera search capabilities, and license plate tracking.
- **2021**: NYPD publishes the Domain Awareness System Impact and Use Policy to define use cases, access controls, and retention limits.
